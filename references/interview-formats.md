## 1. Panel Interviews (2–3 interviewers at once)

**What it is:** Multiple interviewers present simultaneously — common at banks, government,
large corporations, and hospitals.

**Why students fear it:** It feels like an interrogation. Eyes from multiple directions
creates pressure.

**What to know:**
- Each interviewer usually owns specific question categories (one does behavioral, one technical,
  one culture fit)
- Direct your answer to the person who asked, but make brief eye contact with others too
- When answering, say the questioner's name once: "That's a great question, Sarah —
  in that situation I..." (shows you're tracking who asked what)
- If two interviewers seem to disagree on something, stay neutral — don't pick sides

**How to prepare:**
- Same prep as a 1-on-1, but practice "scanning" eye contact (not staring at just one person)
- Bring notes or water to avoid nervous fidgeting

## 2. Technical Screen / Live Coding

**What it is:** A coding challenge done live, usually via HackerRank, Codility, LeetCode-style
platforms, or a shared screen. Sometimes done with an interviewer watching in real time.

**The golden rule:** Think out loud. Always.

**Why:** The interviewer is evaluating your PROCESS, not just the final answer. A student
who talks through their thinking and arrives at 80% of a solution is often rated higher than
one who silently produces a perfect solution.

**Structure for every problem:**
1. **Restate the problem:** "So if I understand correctly, we need to [X] given [Y]?"
2. **Clarify assumptions:** "Should I handle empty inputs? Is the array sorted?"
3. **Talk through your approach before coding:** "I'm thinking of using a hash map because..."
4. **Code with narration:** "Here I'm initializing the counter to zero because..."
5. **Test out loud:** "Let me trace through this with [example input]..."
6. **Discuss trade-offs:** "This is O(n) time — if memory was the constraint I'd approach it differently"

**If you get stuck:**
> "I'm going to take a moment to think through this differently — bear with me."
Then think through a simpler version, a brute force, or a related problem you know.

**Language tips for ESL students:**
- "Let me think through this step by step..."
- "My initial thought is... but I want to verify that..."
- "I'm not 100% sure about the exact syntax, but the logic would be..."

## 3. Case Interviews (Consulting, Finance, Strategy Roles)

**What it is:** A business problem presented verbally or on paper — the student has to
reason through it in real time. Common at McKinsey, BCG, Deloitte, and strategy co-op roles.

**What they're testing:** Structured thinking, communication, comfort with ambiguity,
basic business/math reasoning.

**The standard structure (MECE Framework):**
1. **Clarify:** Ask 2–3 quick clarifying questions before diving in
2. **Structure:** Say "I'd like to think about this across three areas: [X, Y, Z]"
3. **Analyze:** Work through each area systematically, thinking out loud
4. **Conclude:** "Based on this, my recommendation would be... because..."

**Key phrases:**
- *"Let me structure my thinking..."*
- *"Can I take 30 seconds to organize my thoughts?"*
- *"I'd want to understand more about [X] before concluding, but based on what we have..."*
- *"My hypothesis is... and here's how I'd test it..."*

**Mental math tips:**
- Round numbers to make calculation easier and say so: "I'll round to 10M for simplicity..."
- Show your work verbally — accuracy matters less than approach

## 4. Group Interviews

**What it is:** Multiple candidates interviewed simultaneously — common at large retailers
(Shoppers, RBC, TD, government programs), some consulting firms, and rotational programs.

**What they're testing:** Leadership, collaboration, listening, and how you behave in a group.

**The traps:**
- Trying to dominate → looks aggressive, not leadership
- Staying completely silent → looks disengaged
- Being too agreeable → looks like you have no original thinking

**The winning balance:**
- Contribute meaningfully 3–4 times, not constantly
- Build on others' ideas: "That's a really good point — and I'd add that..."
- Invite quieter members: "I'd love to hear what [name] thinks about this part"
- Summarize or synthesize when things get chaotic: "So it sounds like we're aligning on..."

**Key phrases:**
- *"Building on what [name] said..."*
- *"One thing I want to make sure we consider is..."*
- *"To summarize where we are: [X, Y, Z]..."*

## 5. Quick Comparison Table

| Format | Main thing tested | Key survival tip |
|--------|------------------|-----------------|
| Panel (2–3 interviewers) | All competencies, evaluated by a team | Make eye contact with everyone, not just the questioner |
| Technical Screen / Live coding | Problem-solving process | Think out loud — always |
| Case Interview | Structured thinking, business reasoning | Structure before answering — pause and organize |
| Group Interview | Collaboration, leadership, listening | Contribute meaningfully 3–4 times; build on others |
| Standard 1-on-1 | All competencies | The default — use full Phase 4–6 prep |
