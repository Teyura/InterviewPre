## 1. General Communication Style

- **Direct but warm:** Canadians value clarity and honesty, always with politeness.
  Hedging too much ("maybe I think possibly...") reads as uncertain. Being clear is respectful.
- **Small talk is normal:** Interviewers often open with "How's your day going?" or
  "Did you find us okay?" — respond warmly, not formally. "Really well, thanks! I've been
  looking forward to this conversation" is perfect.
- **First names:** Most Canadian workplaces use first names even with senior people.
  If the interviewer introduces themselves as "Sarah," it's fine to call them Sarah.
- **Humor:** Light, genuine humor is fine if it comes naturally — but don't force it.
  When in doubt, professional warmth is always safe.

## 2. Self-Promotion Norms

This is one of the biggest cultural adjustments for international students.

In many cultures, saying "I'm proud of this" or "I did this well" is considered boastful.
**In Canadian interviews, it is expected and professional.**

When an interviewer asks "Why should we hire you?" or "What are your strengths?", they
genuinely want you to advocate for yourself. Staying too humble will hurt your chances —
not because it's wrong, but because it doesn't give them the information they need.

**Frame for students:** "Stating your value clearly is not bragging — it's helping
the interviewer do their job. They WANT to find out if you're a good fit."

Encourage students to practice phrases like:
- *"I'm proud of the fact that..."*
- *"One thing I do particularly well is..."*
- *"I believe I'd bring value in [area] because..."*

## 3. Natural Canadian Professional Phrases

| Instead of... | Try... |
|---------------|--------|
| "I am very hardworking person" | "I put a lot of effort into my work" |
| "I have much experience in..." | "I have experience with..." |
| "I will do my best" | "I'm committed to delivering strong results" |
| "I don't have weakness" | "One area I'm still developing is X, and here's how I'm working on it..." |
| "I know everything about this" | "I have a solid foundation in X and I'm always building on it" |
| "I am not sure but maybe..." | "My understanding is X — I'd want to verify that with [source]" |
| "Thank you for this chance" | "Thank you for your time — I really enjoyed this conversation" |

## 4. Handling Common Awkward Moments

**"I need a moment to think"**
It's 100% professional to say: *"That's a great question — give me just a moment to think."*
Silence for 3–5 seconds is completely normal and reads as thoughtfulness, not weakness.
Many students from fast-paced interview cultures feel they must respond instantly — not here.

**"I didn't understand the question"**
Perfectly fine to say: *"Could you clarify what you mean by [part of the question]?"*
or *"Just to make sure I'm understanding — are you asking about [X]?"*

**"I gave a bad answer and I know it"**
Recovery move — interviewers respect this:
*"Actually, let me rephrase that — I think a better example would be [X]."*
This shows self-awareness and confidence, not weakness.

**"I don't know the answer to a technical question"**
Use the formula:
*"I haven't worked with that specific [tool/concept] directly, but my approach would be
to [reasoning]. I'd start by [action] to understand it, and I'd confirm by [testing/checking].
Based on my experience with [related thing], I'm confident I'd get up to speed quickly."*

## 5. Virtual Interview Etiquette

- **Eye contact = camera, not your video box.** Looking at the camera reads as confident;
  looking at your own face reads as distracted.
- **Nod and react** while the interviewer talks. Silence on video can feel like disconnection.
- **Test everything 30 minutes before:** Zoom/Teams link, camera, mic, lighting.
- **Lighting:** Light source should be IN FRONT of you (facing you), not behind you.
  A window in front of you is perfect. A window behind you makes you a silhouette.
- **Background:** Keep it clean. A neutral wall, a bookshelf, or a professional virtual
  background all work. Avoid messy rooms or distracting posters.
- **If tech fails:** Stay calm. Say "I'm sorry, I had a connection issue — could you
  repeat the last part?" Interviewers are very understanding about this.

## 6. Work Authorization — What to Say

If asked "Are you eligible to work in Canada?", here are clean answers:

**Co-op student with a co-op work permit:**
> "Yes — as a co-op student at [University], I'm eligible to work full-time during
> my work term under my co-op work permit."

**Student with an open work permit (e.g. PGWP or SOWP):**
> "Yes — I hold an open work permit that allows me to work for any employer in Canada."

**Student currently on study permit (no work permit yet):**
> "I'm currently on a study permit. I'm in the process of applying for [co-op work permit /
> PGWP] and would have full work authorization by [start date]."

When in doubt, confirm your specific permit type with your school's international student
office BEFORE the interview.

## 7. Thank-You Email (After the Interview)

Send within **24 hours** of the interview. Keep it short — 3–4 sentences.
Canadian employers appreciate this; it's a professional norm, not pushy.

**Template:**
> Subject: Thank You — [Role Name] Interview
>
> Dear [Interviewer's First Name],
>
> Thank you so much for taking the time to meet with me today about the [Role] co-op position.
> I really enjoyed learning about [something specific from the conversation] — it made me
> even more excited about the possibility of contributing to your team.
>
> I look forward to hearing about next steps. Please don't hesitate to reach out if you
> need anything further from me.
>
> Best regards,
> [Student Name]

**Key tips:**
- Use their first name (not "Dear Sir/Madam")
- Mention ONE specific thing from the interview — shows you were listening
- Keep it short — don't re-pitch yourself; that's what the interview was for
