## 1. What STAR Is and Why It Works

STAR gives a behavioral answer a clear shape so the interviewer can follow the story
and evaluate the specific competency being tested.

| Letter | Stands For | Target length | Key question to answer |
|--------|------------|---------------|------------------------|
| **S** | Situation | 1–2 sentences | What was the context? |
| **T** | Task | 1–2 sentences | What was YOUR responsibility specifically? |
| **A** | Action | 3–5 sentences | What did YOU personally do, step by step? |
| **R** | Result | 1–2 sentences | What happened because of your actions? |

The Action is the heart of the answer — it should be the longest part.
The Situation is often over-explained by students — coach them to keep it brief.

**Total target: 120–160 words written, ~90 seconds spoken**

## 2. The Most Common STAR Mistakes (and How to Fix Them)

| Mistake | How it shows up | Coaching fix |
|---------|-----------------|--------------|
| **"We" instead of "I"** | "Our team decided to..." "We built..." | "Tell me what YOU specifically did — not the team." |
| **No Result** | Answer ends at the action step | "What happened as a result? Even a small positive outcome counts." |
| **Vague Result** | "It went well", "the project was successful" | "Can you give one specific detail? A grade, time saved, feedback received?" |
| **Over-explaining Situation** | 5+ sentences on background before getting to what they did | "Let's trim the setup to 1–2 sentences so you have more time on your actions." |
| **Too short** | Only 2–3 sentences total | "This is a strong start — can you walk me through exactly what steps you took?" |
| **Too long / rambling** | 4+ minutes of talking | "Great content — let's tighten to ~90 seconds. What's the single most important action?" |
| **No personal agency** | "Things just worked out" / "My teammates helped a lot" | "What did YOU decide? What was the choice or action that was yours?" |

## 3. Prompts for Students Who Get Stuck on Examples

Many international students panic when asked for examples, especially if they feel their
experience is "not enough." Use these prompts to help them surface stories:

**For teamwork/conflict:**
- "Think about any group project in school — even a class assignment counts."
- "Has there been a time a teammate didn't do their part? What did you do?"
- "Have you ever had to convince someone to do something differently?"

**For leadership:**
- "Have you ever organized anything — an event, a study group, a project timeline?"
- "Is there a time a teammate came to you for help or advice?"
- "Have you ever stepped up when no one was taking charge?"

**For pressure/challenge:**
- "Think about a time something went wrong — a bug, a deadline, a misunderstanding."
- "Has there been a moment where you had to pivot quickly?"

**For learning quickly:**
- "Think about a time you had to use a tool or skill you didn't know before."
- "Was there a course that was really difficult — how did you handle it?"

**For mistakes:**
- "Think of a time something didn't go as planned. What did you do next?"
- "Has a professor or supervisor ever given you feedback you had to act on?"

**Key message for students:** Co-op and school experience is 100% valid.
Hackathons, class projects, clubs, part-time jobs, and volunteering all count.
You do NOT need previous full-time work experience.

## 4. Feedback Language Bank

Use these phrases when giving mock interview feedback — they're direct but kind:

**Encouraging:**
- "That's a really solid foundation — here's what would make it excellent..."
- "You actually had a great story there — let's just sharpen the ending."
- "The Situation and Task were really clear. Let's build up the Action."
- "I could see the effort you put in — here's how to make that visible to the interviewer."

**Redirecting:**
- "I notice you said 'we' throughout — can you tell me what YOU personally did?"
- "You ended right before the best part — what was the outcome?"
- "This is good content, but it's running a bit long. If you had 20 seconds less, what would you cut?"
- "The interviewer is really going to want to hear [specific missing element] here."

**Validating difficulty:**
- "This is one of the hardest question types — you're doing well."
- "Answering in your second language while structuring a story is genuinely hard. Give yourself credit."

## 5. How to Coach Results Without Numbers

Many students (especially those without formal work experience) struggle to quantify results.
Help them find qualitative equivalents:

| Instead of needing a number, accept: |
|--------------------------------------|
| "The professor gave our team the highest mark in the class." |
| "My teammate said it was the clearest explanation they'd heard." |
| "We shipped the feature on time, which hadn't happened the previous semester." |
| "The club president asked me to lead the next project because of how I handled it." |
| "I was the only first-year student selected for the committee." |

Teach students: **Social proof IS a result.** Praise from a professor, selection for a role,
positive peer feedback — these are all valid outcomes.
